Ouch! Mahogany knees, burnt thighs and very red heads: Bike-mad Instagram users show the (painful) results of cycling in the sun

Plenty of us enjoy flashing some flesh in the summer months and many are guilty of forgetting to slap on the SPF - and bike-mad Instagram users are keen to show off the, sometimes painful, results.

Sporty social media fans are developing deep mahogany tans on the exposed parts of their legs while their upper thighs and ankles - usually covered by cycling shorts and socks - remain white and pasty.

But rather than shy away from the camera or attempt to even out the faux pas with some Fake Bake they're wearing their tan lines with pride by posting photos all over Instagram.

Some are even enhancing their contrasting skin colours with fancy filters.

Others, meanwhile, suffer red raw sunburn on their legs or even on their scalp - complete with zebra stripes from their bike helmet.

And with temperatures rising and Tour De France starting on Saturday, the trend is unlikely to burn out.

So to keep your skin safe if you're getting on your bike, slap on the SPF.

Professional-looking cyclists are developing deep mahogany tans on the exposed parts of their legs

Maulik Jhaveri, Service Development Pharmacist at LloydsPharmacy says: 'Over exposure to suns UV rays is the major cause of sunburn and increases the risk of skin cancer. While you are cycling through various terrains, you are likely to be exposed to varying intensity and duration of sun increasing the risk of sunburn. 

'Thighs, arms, face and areas of the head not covered by the helmet are most likely to burn. The best way to prevent sunburn while cycling out in the sun is to apply an appropriate sun protection factor sunscreen, minimum SPF 15 for adults and SPF 30 for children. 

'Applying sunscreen just once, while you are out the whole day will not protect you from sun damage. It is very important to reapply sunscreen at regular intervals, usually every two hours especially while cycling as your body is likely to sweat a lot removing the protective layer of sunscreen. Sunscreens formulated specifically for various parts of the body are now readily available and can prevent that icky feeling, say for example if applied on the head.'
